Historical Background

In a letter from George Fredk. Koehler which was written in April 20th, 1785, the cannon was described as a revolutionary military device because of its depressing carriage which allowed it to be aimed at a greater variety of angles, from 70° depression to 45° elevation. This made it stood out compared to other standard guns which were very limited in range. For the crew, loading was made safe and easy by a spindle that turned the cannon sideways. This allowed one person to load the shot while staying protected. Most importantly, the cannon's design allowed it to recoil on a slide and return automatically to its original firing position, eliminating the heavy manual labor of repositioning the gun after every shot. This accurate, straight-line recoil was key to its percise accuracy, which made it effective at consistently hitting small targets over very long distances.

"A gun upon the depositing carriage can be fired at an angle of 70° at depression... whereas a common one can not be fired at greater depression than 10°."

The design allowed soldiers to load safely under cover and maintain accuracy. In tests, 58 of 60 shots hit a target 1400 yards away.

How It Worked

  • Rotated on a spindle for easy loading
  • Could fire downward from elevated positions
  • Sliding bed absorbed recoil
  • Returned to exact position after firing
