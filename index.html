The Limits of Empire: Resistance and Ingenuity

A Public History Exploration of Anti-Colonial Action in Early North America (c. 1650–1800)

The Spectrum of Anti-Colonial Action

Historians define **anti-colonialism** not as a single act of rebellion, but as a broad spectrum of strategies—from armed revolt to diplomatic negotiation to mere survival—all challenging imperial domination and exposing the limits of colonial power.

This project explores how Indigenous and African-descended peoples resisted European empires (Spanish, French, British) in the 17th and 18th centuries.

Revolt: Armed Uprisings

The most direct challenge to imperial control. The **Pueblo Revolt of 1680** stands as a powerful example: an armed uprising that successfully expelled the Spanish from New Mexico for a period of time. Similarly, the **First Maroon War** in Jamaica, led by the formerly enslaved African woman **Queen Nannie**, fought for and won the freedom and recognition of a distinct Maroon group from the British.

Diplomacy: Negotiation and Sovereignty

Resistance could also be negotiated. The **Great Peace of Montreal in 1701** was a moment of intense diplomacy between the French Empire and Indigenous peoples, primarily the Haudenosaunee. This negotiation profoundly reshaped the political relationships in northeastern North America, limiting French imperial power through collective agreement.

The Enduring Struggle

These early movements reveal enduring struggles over **land, sovereignty, and freedom**. This resistance continues today, as seen in modern protest movements like "Land Back" and critiques of "racial capitalism," which challenge the inherited structures and memories of empire.

The Engine of Empire: Precision and Power

The projection of colonial power relied on technological and military superiority. The document describes an ingenious, highly accurate field piece—a gun on a **depressing carriage**—used during the Siege of Gibraltar (1779–1783), capable of firing at an extreme **70° depression**.

Its unique fixed recoil mechanism allowed for **repeated shots at the same depression with extreme accuracy**, with one officer reporting 28 of 30 shots hitting a single traverse from a distance of nearly 1400 yards.
