The Historical Paradox

The 1785 letter describing the Koehler Depressing Carriage Gun represents the apex of British military-technological prowess—a machine of precise, repeatable destruction. Yet, the history of North America before 1800 demonstrates a profound historical paradox: every attempt to preserve the empire through increased discipline and control ultimately produced its own undoing. This exhibit contrasts the cold mechanics of imperial power with the dynamic, enduring forces of anti-colonial resistance.

Exhibit 1: The Precision of Revolt (Pueblo Revolt, 1680)

The ability of the cannon to hit a single traverse 28 out of 30 times from 1400 yards highlights its devastating accuracy. But how effective is this precision against unified, armed rebellion? The Pueblo Revolt proves that organized Indigenous action could entirely expel a colonial power, rendering the technology of control inert. Revolt, not artillery, defined the limits of Spanish imperial aspiration.

Exhibit 2: The Diplomacy of Defense (Great Peace of Montreal, 1701)

The Koehler gun's innovation was the ability to return to the "exact same direction, and depression it did before firing," ensuring mechanical repeatability. In contrast, the Indigenous nations at the Great Peace of Montreal achieved a non-mechanical, *enduring* repeatability—peace and autonomy through complex, strategic diplomacy that outlasted the effectiveness of French and British cannons. Diplomacy was the superior technology for maintaining sovereignty.

Exhibit 3: The Angle of Freedom (First Maroon War, 1739)

The cannon's unique ability to fire at a **70° depression** was designed for tactical advantage, such as shooting from high-ground positions or firing upon enemies below a precipice. Ironically, the Maroons in Jamaica used the high ground itself—the Cockpit Country—to establish a refuge that successfully resisted British military campaigns. Their "acts of resistance" forced the British to recognize the limits of their control and grant them treaty freedom.
